01 - Honesty Step One

Honesty

Verb shape - honest-ing

Honesty as a verb is admitting what is real: "This is where I actually am." It is saying, "I can't manage this," instead of "I've got it." It is telling the truth about our drinking, our fear, our resentments, our money, our food, our scrolling - not in theory, but out loud to another person. It is choosing not to bend the story, not to leave out the ugly parts, not to exaggerate the good.

Root & seed: Honesty is the root that breaks the hard shell of denial so anything else can grow. Without it, hope has nowhere to land. When we tell the full truth, even shakily, we create a small opening where the idea of help, change, or a different future can slip in. That crack in the old story is the seedbed where hope-ing begins.

In daily life:

  • At home: admitting, "I'm afraid," instead of acting angry or silent.
  • At work: saying, "I messed that up," before anyone has to chase you.
  • In your head: catching the spin and telling your sponsor or trusted friend the unedited version.

02 - Hope Step Two

Hope

Verb shape - hope-ing

Hope as a verb is allowing a different outcome to be possible. It is letting the sentence "Maybe it does not have to stay like this" finish in your heart. It is reaching toward help instead of assuming failure. Hope does not demand that we feel positive; it asks that we keep the door cracked for something better than self-will and chaos.

Root & seed: Honesty cracks the surface; hope is the first light that comes through the crack. Because we have admitted the truth, we are no longer protecting the old way, so we can consider a Power greater than ourselves. That consideration - however small - becomes the bridge to faith. Hope plants the seed that maybe, just maybe, we can trust something beyond our own broken management.

In daily life:

  • At home: saying, "Let's try something different this week," instead of "Nothing ever changes."
  • At work: asking for guidance instead of quietly drowning in a task.
  • In your head: replacing "What is the point?" with "What if this is exactly where change starts?"

03 - Faith Step Three

Faith

Verb shape - faith-ing

Faith as a verb is trusting enough to move your feet. It is turning over your will and life not as a slogan but as choices: what you do with your time, your body, your phone, your money. It is acting "as if" your Higher Power is real and present, even when feelings are flat or scared.

Root & seed: Hope stretches us toward the idea that help exists; faith puts weight on that help. Because hope has softened our grip, we can begin to say, "Okay, I will follow instead of drive." That surrender in action grows courage. When we act on faith - show up, call, confess, change course - we discover that fear does not have to be in charge. That discovery is the seed of courage-ing.

In daily life:

  • At home: praying before reacting, then choosing a calm response instead of a blast.
  • At work: doing the next honest task even when fear says, "Hide."
  • In your head: saying, "God, you take this," and then not rehearsing the argument for the next hour.

04 - Courage Step Four

Courage

Verb shape - courage-ing

Courage as a verb is walking into what you would rather avoid. It is putting pen to paper when the inventory feels impossible. It is dialing the phone when your stomach flips. Courage does not erase fear; it moves with fear toward the truth. It looks like standing still when you want to run, or speaking up when you want to disappear.

Root & seed: Faith says, "I will follow"; courage tests what happens when we actually do. Leaning on faith, we can face our history, motives, and patterns without collapsing. That honest, sustained look at ourselves is what grows integrity. Courage plants the seed of integrity-ing by refusing to leave our darkness in the dark.

In daily life:

  • At home: starting the hard conversation without needing the other person's response to be perfect.
  • At work: owning your part in a conflict instead of building a case in your head.
  • In your head: writing the resentment or fear inventory instead of doom-scrolling.

05 - Integrity Step Five

Integrity

Verb shape - integrity-ing

Integrity as a verb is aligning what we say, what we do, and what we really believe. It is bringing into the light the full story with another human being. It is choosing not to live as separate versions of ourselves: one in public, one in private, one in our head. Integrity is not perfection; it is consistently returning to wholeness after we drift.

Root & seed: Courage lets us face the truth; integrity lets us share it and then act from it. As we walk through Step Five, we stop curating our image and allow another person, and our Higher Power, to see it all. That alignment - inside and outside matching - loosens our grip on old defects. This loosening is the seed of willing-ing to be changed.

In daily life:

  • At home: apologizing without excuses and then changing the behavior schedule, not just the words.
  • At work: refusing to gossip about someone you just smiled at in the meeting.
  • In your head: catching dishonesty quickly and telling on yourself instead of quietly carrying it.

06 - Willingness Step Six

Willingness

Verb shape - willing-ing

Willingness as a verb is opening your hands. It is stopping the quiet defense of your favorite defects - the ones that "protect" you - and saying, "I am ready to be changed here, too." It is trying new spiritual actions, even when they feel small or awkward. Willingness is letting your heart be softened where it was once locked.

Root & seed: Integrity lines up our story with reality; willingness allows that reality to be altered. Because we have seen and spoken our patterns, we can now consent to have them removed, not just managed. That consent - even if it is "I am willing to be willing" - becomes the soil for humility. Willingness plants the seed of humble-ing by admitting we cannot fix ourselves but we can cooperate with the One who can.

In daily life:

  • At home: listening to feedback from family without instantly arguing your case.
  • At work: accepting a new way of doing the job instead of secretly clinging to the old.
  • In your head: noticing a familiar defect and praying, "Please change this in me," instead of giving it a free pass.

07 - Humility Step Seven

Humility

Verb shape - humble-ing

Humility as a verb is standing at your true size. It is neither shrinking into worthlessness nor inflating into superiority. It is asking for help where you are weak and accepting your gifts without apology. Humility is listening, learning, and letting your Higher Power be the center instead of your ego.

Root & seed: Willingness cracks our pride; humility lets grace fill the crack. When we honestly offer our shortcomings to be removed, we move from self-improvement to God-improvement. That posture of right-sized-ness opens the channel for love to flow outward. Humility plants the seed of love-ing by clearing away the self-obsession that blocked it.

In daily life:

  • At home: saying, "You're right, I overreacted," without a but.
  • At work: being teachable, even when you're the most experienced in the room.
  • In your head: remembering you are one of many, not the star or the villain of every story.

08 - Love Step Eight

Love

Verb shape - love-ing

Love as a verb is seeking the good of another, not just feeling warmly toward them. It is preparing to repair the harm we have done. It is letting compassion, not shame or self-righteousness, drive our amends list. Love looks like seeing the person behind the behavior - including ourselves - and treating them as someone worth healing with.

Root & seed: Humility makes room for other people to matter as much as we do; love steps in to act on their behalf. As we become willing to make amends, we stop using others as props in our drama and start seeing them as souls we have harmed. That steady, compassionate attention energizes discipline. Love plants the seed of discipline-ing by giving us a reason to show up consistently, not just when we feel like it.

In daily life:

  • At home: asking, "How can I make this right?" instead of defending why you did it.
  • At work: treating coworkers as fellow travelers rather than obstacles or tools.
  • In your head: speaking to yourself the way you would speak to a shaky newcomer.

09 - Discipline Step Nine

Discipline

Verb shape - discipline-ing

Discipline as a verb is showing up on purpose. It is following through on amends plans, not just making lists about them. It is keeping simple spiritual routines whether or not the mood is there. Discipline is choosing small, faithful actions over grand emotional speeches.

Root & seed: Love gives us the desire to repair; discipline provides the structure to actually do it. When we keep showing up - for amends, for practices, for people - our character starts to take shape in new ways. That steady rhythm trains us to stay with the process when it is no longer exciting. Discipline plants the seed of persevere-ing by proving that change is built from many plain days of doing the next right thing.

In daily life:

  • At home: following through on being present at dinner, not just promising "more family time."
  • At work: arriving when you said you would and doing the task to the best of your ability, again and again.
  • In your head: keeping your commitment to prayer, inventory, or meetings on boring days.

10 - Perseverance Step Ten

Perseverance

Verb shape - persevere-ing

Perseverance as a verb is continuing after the shine wears off. It is doing daily inventory when nothing dramatic is happening. It is restarting after a slip, a resentment, or a bad day without turning it into a reason to quit. Perseverance is staying with the work long enough to see the longer pattern.

Root & seed: Discipline builds rhythm; perseverance extends that rhythm through storms and dry spells. As we keep sweeping our side of the street, we start catching trouble earlier and earlier - before it explodes. That growing steadiness sharpens our spiritual radar. Perseverance plants the seed of aware-ing by keeping us awake to what is actually going on inside and around us.

In daily life:

  • At home: continuing to practice new communication habits even after an argument flares up.
  • At work: staying honest and kind through busy seasons, not just easy weeks.
  • In your head: noticing when you want to give up and instead saying, "Just the next right thing, for now."

11 - Awareness Step Eleven

Awareness

Verb shape - aware-ing

Awareness as a verb is listening on purpose. It is pausing to check in with God, with yourself, and with the moment before you react. It is watching your motives like a dashboard - not to shame yourself, but to steer. Awareness includes prayer, meditation, and the simple act of noticing: "What am I feeling? What am I believing? What is being asked of me here?"

Root & seed: Perseverance keeps us engaged long enough for us to see patterns; awareness turns those patterns into guidance. As we practice conscious contact, we become more sensitive to nudges, warnings, and invitations from our Higher Power. That clarity naturally moves outward into service. Awareness plants the seed of serving by showing us where we are actually needed, not just where we want to look good.

In daily life:

  • At home: pausing to pray or breathe before you answer the hard question or snarky comment.
  • At work: noticing when ego, fear, or people-pleasing starts driving your choices.
  • In your head: regularly asking, "What is my part here? What guidance am I ignoring?"

12 - Service Step Twelve

Service

Verb shape - serving

Service as a verb is carrying the message by living it. It is doing what helped you: chairing, sponsoring, making coffee, giving rides, sharing honestly - not because you are better, but because you are not doing it alone anymore. It is bringing the principles into rooms that are not labeled AA: kitchen tables, job sites, traffic, group texts.

Root & seed: Awareness shows us where love and honesty are needed; service moves our feet into those spaces. As we give away what we have been given, all the previous principles get exercised again: honesty in what we share, hope in what we offer, faith in the Power behind it, courage to speak, integrity to live it, willingness to be inconvenienced, humility to remember we are still learners, love to stay gentle, discipline and perseverance to keep showing up, awareness to listen as we go. Service plants the seed back at honest-ing by reminding us of where we came from and keeping us close to the newcomer who is living our old story.

In daily life:

  • At home: quietly doing the next loving thing without needing credit.
  • At work: practicing these principles in how you talk, decide, and treat people, not just in what you say about AA.
  • In your head: remembering, "I stay sober and sane by giving this away," especially when you want to isolate.
